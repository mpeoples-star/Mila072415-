Scene Setting · Context
The Challenge: Access Isn't Adoption
On-Screen Content
📉
Platform rollout — Month 3
Active users: ~40% · Support tickets: high · Manual workarounds: common
A district of 255 staff had a new digital platform. They had login credentials, a quick-start guide, and a one-hour launch session. Three months in, adoption had stalled below 50%.
The tool wasn't the problem. The system around it was.
Narration Script
"When we launched the platform, we did what most organizations do: we gave people access and called it onboarding. We sent a guide. We ran a session. And then we waited. What we got back were the same support tickets, week after week. The same features going unused. The same workarounds. Access had happened. Adoption hadn't."
Pause for reflection: "Before we look at what changed, take a moment: what does adoption actually mean in your context? Not just usage — confident, habitual use?"

Core Concept · Categorization
Not All Resistance Looks the Same
On-Screen Content
Before designing any solution, I needed to understand why people weren't adopting — because the reason matters. Three distinct failure modes emerged:
🚫
Avoidance
Never meaningfully onboarded. Avoided the platform entirely. Often high-anxiety users or those with negative past tech experiences.
🏄
Surface Use
Completed setup and used 1–2 basic features. Never explored what the platform could do for their specific role.
📉
Regression
Started well, then slipped back to manual habits 60–90 days post-onboarding. No reinforcement system to hold the change.
Knowledge Check · Scenario Question
A team member completed onboarding six weeks ago and was using the platform regularly. Now they've stopped logging in and gone back to spreadsheets. Which failure mode does this represent?
✓ Correct. This is regression — the adoption happened, but wasn't sustained. The intervention needed here is reinforcement, not re-onboarding.
Not quite. Look at the timeline: they were using it, then stopped. That points to regression — adoption that wasn't reinforced. Re-onboarding them from scratch would miss the real problem.

Process · Five-Phase System
Building the System: Five Phases
On-Screen Content
Each failure mode required a different intervention. The response wasn't a single training event — it was a system with five interdependent phases:
1
Diagnose First
Map failure modes before designing solutions. Data + conversation, not assumptions.
2
Role-Based Pathways
Replace generic onboarding with role-specific entry points tied to real tasks.
3
Self-Service Knowledge System
Make finding the answer faster than submitting a ticket. Modular, task-based assets.
4
Reinforcement Layer
Timed follow-up content to prevent regression. Tied to natural workflow moments.
5
AI Integration
Embed AI-supported workflows to reduce effort and model confident AI use.
Narration Script
"The core shift was this: instead of asking 'how do we get people to use the platform,' we asked 'what's actually getting in their way?' Once we understood the failure modes, we could design to each one specifically."
"Avoidance needed a low-stakes entry point and an early win. Surface use needed role relevance — connecting the tool to the exact work they already did. Regression needed a system that didn't rely on willpower. Each of these is a design problem, not a motivation problem."
🔄

Deep Dive · AI Integration
Where AI Fit In
On-Screen Content
AI wasn't added to the enablement program as an afterthought — it was woven into the system in two distinct ways:
AS A CONTENT TOOL
AI tools (Claude, ChatGPT) were used to accelerate the development of learning assets — drafting job aids, generating scenario variations, and personalizing examples by role. This reduced content development time significantly.
AS A WORKFLOW LAYER
AI-supported guided workflows were built directly into the user experience — helping staff complete complex tasks with less cognitive load. The goal was to reduce hesitation by showing concrete, role-specific application rather than abstract capability.
Narration Script
"The biggest barrier to AI adoption isn't skepticism — it's uncertainty. People don't know where to start. The question I kept hearing was: 'what would I even use it for?' So we answered it specifically. Not 'AI can help with writing' — but 'here's the exact prompt to draft your parent communication in under 2 minutes.'"
"When you give people a concrete workflow instead of a capability overview, the activation energy drops dramatically. That's the design principle."
🤖
AI workflow example
"Here is the task. Here is the prompt. Here is what you do with the output."
Reflection Activity
Apply it to your context
Think of one task your team does regularly that takes more time than it should. How might an AI-supported workflow reduce friction for that specific task?

Summary · Application
What This Means for Your Work
Key Takeaways
  • Diagnose before you design. Not all adoption failure looks the same. The intervention must match the failure mode.
  • Make the right behavior easier than the wrong one. Self-service should be faster than asking for help. AI workflows should reduce, not add, cognitive load.
  • Onboarding is not an event. Adoption is a system — entry, reinforcement, and feedback loops working together.
  • AI adoption follows the same rules. Show people what to do with it, not what it can do in theory.
